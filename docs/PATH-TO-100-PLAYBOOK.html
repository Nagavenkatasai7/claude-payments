Realistic answer: ~90% production‑ready after the fixes — and 100% is neither achievable nor desirable.

You can take resilience to ~90% and scale to ~95% with well‑understood patterns and a few weeks of work. Microservices caps at ~78–80% — and that cap is correct: the remaining ~20% is the ACID money core (settlement.ts:43-69) plus shared reference data (Partners, FX). Forcing those into separate services replaces ACID with sagas / eventual consistency — the one trade a remittance product must not make.

Irreducible ~8–12%: a region‑wide cloud outage, the SLAs of external deps (WhatsApp, Ollama, the funding PSP), and the transactional core. Those are architectural laws, not bugs.

Irreducible ~20–22% — keep it monolithic ON PURPOSE

The money core — beginSettlement() flips transfers.status→paid AND inserts the outbox rows in one ACID transaction (settlement.ts:43-69), with claim‑first minting vs idempotency_keys. Splitting it forces a Saga with compensating transactions = eventual consistency on money (you'd tell a customer "paid," then can't un‑tell them). Partners + FX are shared reference data. This ~20% is an architectural law — the right answer is to NOT split it.

✅ Do (highest ROI)

  • Close the outbox leaks & the session SPOF — most of "won't fall over" for ~2 weeks of work.
  • Pool connections + cache/pre‑aggregate the read paths — 10× users almost free.
  • Extract the genuinely‑decoupled edges (types, AI/analytics, provider interfaces) before the agent.

🚫 Don't (negative value)

  • Split beginSettlement / the ledger across services — replaces ACID with sagas on money.
  • Shard Partners or FX per‑service — stale‑data bugs; keep them shared reference data.
  • Chase a literal "100%" — the irreducible ~10–20% is region/SLA/transactional law.

Bottom line: with the fixes above, the system realistically reaches ~90% production‑readiness (resilience ~90–92%, scale ~95% with replicas), and ~78–80% microservices‑decomposition — the ceiling, by design, because the transactional money core must stay one ACID unit. Spend the first two weeks on the quick wins (outbox leaks, sessions, connection pooling, caching) — they buy the biggest jump in "one part fails, the system survives" and "handles 10× the load" for the least risk.
